Basic Information

Definition

Norse Mythology is a pagan religion containing stories and myths about gods, giants, and other creatures. It's purpose was to help people understand the world and guide them during their life.

When?

It was practiced around the time of Viking Age (circa 800 - 1100 CE) before the Christianization of Scandinavia.

Where?

Scandinavia, Britain, Germany and parts of lands populated by Vikings (Greenland, Iceland, etc.).

Sources of information

Main sources are pre-Christian manuscripts from Iceland containing skaldic and Eddic poetry*, sagas and many tales. Apart from them, archaeologist found runic inscriptions with and we can also see Vikings being actively demonized in old Christian texts.

* skaldic poetry = poetry written by skalds; Eddic poetry = poetry from an unknown author written in 13th century about Viking Age

Mythology

Creation of the World

Before the world as we know was created, there was nothing but pure void called Ginnungagap, from which Niflheim and Muspelheim were formed. Niflheim became a land of ice and Muspelheim a land of fire. In the middle of Ginnungagap both lands met and while the fire was melting ice, the first giant was created - Ymir. From his sweat and leg a family of frost giants was formed. They were fed by a giant cow, that later found Buri inside the ice block, that she was licking. Buri is the giant, but also the first God. With his wife he would have three sons - Odin, Vili, and Ve. However, his sons were unhappy with the fact that there were only three of them and the number of giants was constantly growing, so they decided to kill Ymir. But it wasn't that easy. A whole war began, but they managed to kill Ymir. Most of the giants drowned in blood, that was flowing out of his body and only two survived and escaped.

From the Ymir's body, the whole world was created. "The blood became the oceans, rivers, and lakes. The flesh became the land. The bones became the mountains. The teeth were made into rocks. The hair became the grass and trees. The eyelashes became Midgard. They threw the brain up in the air and it became the clouds, and the skull became the sky, Ymir’s skull would be the lid that covered the new world. The brothers grabbed some of the sparks shooting out from Muspelheim, the land of fire. They threw the sparks up toward the inside of the skull, these sparks gleamed at night and this is what we call the stars." (Skjalden, 2011).

Valhalla

The great hall, created by Odin, where all the worthy fallen ones feast, sleep and train for the Ragnarok. It is made of shields and its gates are surrounded by wolves with eagles flying above it. Remaining in the Valhalla after death is the dream of every true Viking. To get there, one must live true viking life and die fighting - dying by old age or sickness would only grant you Hel. Of course, there are a few exceptions, but only Odin himself knows why he granted them.

Ragnarok

Ragnarok is the destruction of all of the worlds and everything that is present in the Mythology. All vikings were familiar with the fate of the world and they were actively preparing for it during their stay in Valhalla. The whole process of Ragnarok is depicted as a cruel, almost never-ending winter with no sun and stars, during which would all evil creatures rise to attack the Gods, who would die. In this enormous chaos, the earth would sink into the sea and almost everything is lost, but not forever. After everything is settled, the earth will rise again. Surprisingly, there are a few survivors - 3 Gods, son of Thor and daughter of the God Sol. They would start building the world again, together with the God Baldur, who would come back from the underworld. The fallen ones should be together in a great hall with golden roof.

Mythical creatures

Apart from the giants that were mentioned before, the Norse Mythology is full of different magical or beast-like creatures. Here is the introduction to a few of the main ones.

Dwarves

Dwarves were created from worms, that were crawling out of the dead giant Ymir. Four of them, called Nordi, Vestri, Sundri and Austri (after cardinal directions), were told to hold up the sky at four different directions of the world. The rest went to the Svartalfheim, which is place near the bottom of the world. They live there in caves or under the ground, and they are visited by other creatures (mostly Gods) who are seeking their skills. Their role in the Old World was for crafting weapons, armor, jewelry, etc. Without them, Thor would not have his Mjolnir, Odin his Gungnir, and many other creatures would miss their iconic, necessary equipment.

Elves

Beautiful tall creatures, that almost didn't interact with humans - Elves. Their role was mainly as healers, but even that did not happen very often. They were ruled by the God Freyr and were divided into two groups, to which we could refer as to "good and evil", or "light and dark".

Valkyries

Another beautiful creatures - the last thing men saw on the battle ground after being covered in blood and on the edge of life. Valkyries were female characters who served Odin. Their role was to decide whether the fallen ones are worthy to continue their life journey in Valhalla, who will go to Hel, or who will remain amongst the living. They also protected humans on their travels. Being able to fly certainly helped them accomplish these tasks. Apart from that, they cared for worthy fallen ones - they poured mead, healed wounds, and brought men back on their feet. That is also why they sometimes appear as their lovers. They were often accompanied by ravens, swans, or horses.

Fenrir

One of the most feared creatures is a giant wolf called Fenrir, son of God Loki. He plays a significant role in the Norse Mythology as he kills Odin during Ragnarok. He broke out from the chains, that were created by the other Gods and dwarves to protect the world, and he bit off hand of the God Tyr after his unsuccessful attempt to tame him.

Jormungandr

The Midnight Serpent is another child of Loki. At first, he was living in the Jotunheim wtih his siblings and mother, but Odin removed him and threw him deep into the ocean (due to his movements, the tidal waves and sea storms were happening), so he would not do any harm to anyone. However, he grew in size so much, he could encircle the whole world of humans while biting his own tail. Thanks to him, the whole Ragnarok began - he let go of his tail, came out of the ocean and poisoned the sky. In the stories we can see him fighting twice with Thor. First time, when the God is trying to fish him so he would slain him with his Mjolnir. He used the head of a giant ox as a bait, but he is stopped by an accompanied giant Hymir, who is scared that the boat will sink and that this act would bring the end to the world. Second time they meet during the Ragnarok, where they both die during their fight. However, the text from Prose Edda is more inclined to the version, in which the serpent was not killed and is still hidden in the depth of the ocean.

Huginn & Muninn

Both Huginn and Muninn are ravens that belong to Odin. They fly above the world of humans and bring the information back to him. Sometimes they are being referred to as thought and memory.
